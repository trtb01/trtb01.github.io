11-21-25 ISS Crew Activities Highlight Science and Fitness

The Expedition 73 crew aboard the International Space Station had a busy week focusing on health and physics experiments.

Activities included using specialized equipment to measure crew mass, installing samples for an experiment observing liquid droplets, and setting up a temporary sleep station for an arriving crew member.

These microgravity studies are crucial for preparing for long-duration space missions and for advancing regenerative medicine back on Earth.

11-21-25 Scientists Get First Detailed Look at a 'Vampire Star'

Scientists have achieved a highly detailed observation of a 'vampire star,' or accreting white dwarf, using X-ray polarimetry.

This pioneering method allowed researchers to make precise measurements of the white dwarf's accretion geometry, where it is feeding on a companion star.

The successful application of X-ray polarimetry opens up new possibilities for studying other types of accreting white dwarfs and black holes that were previously inaccessible to this type of analysis.

11-21-25 BepiColombo Mission Reaches One Year from Mercury Orbit

The joint ESA/JAXA BepiColombo mission marks one year until its arrival at Mercury, having successfully performed multiple flybys of Earth, Venus, and Mercury since its 2018 launch.

The mission has already delivered key scientific data, including measurements of Mercury's magnetic field during its close flights past the small planet.

The main science phase will begin when the two spacecraft enter orbit to study Mercury's interior, surface, exosphere, and magnetic field, furthering our understanding of the Solar System's innermost planet.

11-21-25 ESA-CAS Smile Mission Approved for Spring 2026 Launch

The Solar Wind Magnetosphere Ionosphere Link Explorer (Smile) mission, a collaboration between ESA and the Chinese Academy of Sciences (CAS), has passed its final review and is approved for launch.

This milestone confirms the spacecraft meets all requirements and is now preparing for a lift-off window set for April to May 2026.

Smile is designed to provide the first global, simultaneous view of how Earth's magnetic environment responds to the solar wind, offering new insight into our planet's protective magnetic shield.
